Navigating the Matrix: CLI Basics

Where we're going, we don't need a mouse. | 👤 By JR Nation

When you open a terminal window, you aren't just staring at a black box. You are standing inside a specific physical folder on your computer's hard drive. Without a graphical file manager to show you cute folder icons, you need to use text commands to "look around" and "walk" between rooms.

Mastering these five fundamental commands will give you the power to manipulate your entire system without ever taking your hands off the keyboard.

💡 Practice Makes Perfect: Reading commands is boring. Open the JR Nation Terminal Playground in a new tab and actually type these commands out as you read this guide.

1. Where am I? (pwd)

If you ever get lost in the terminal, you need to ask it where you are currently standing. pwd stands for Print Working Directory. It will output your exact location on the hard drive.

$ pwd

/home/jrnation/Downloads

2. What is in here? (ls)

Now that you know where you are, you want to look at the files around you. ls stands for List. It is the equivalent of opening a folder and looking at the icons.

  • ls — Lists the standard, visible files and folders.
  • ls -a — Lists all files, including the hidden Dotfiles we talked about in the Essentials guide.
  • ls -la — The pro move. Lists all files in a vertical format, showing exactly who owns them, when they were modified, and how large they are.

3. How do I move? (cd)

You know where you are, and you see a folder you want to enter. You use cd, which stands for Change Directory.

$ cd Documents

# Moves you forward into the Documents folder.


$ cd ..

# Moves you backwards exactly one room (up one level).


$ cd ~

# The panic button. Teleports you instantly back to your /home folder.

4. Creating Files & Folders (touch, mkdir)

No more right-clicking and selecting "New Folder". In the terminal, creation is instantaneous.

  • mkdir secret_folder — (Make Directory) Instantly creates a new folder in your current location.
  • touch notes.txt — Instantly creates an empty text file.

5. Deleting (rm) & The Danger Zone

The Linux terminal does not have a "Recycle Bin". When you delete something via the command line, it is gone forever. Treat these commands with extreme respect.

  • rm notes.txt — (Remove) Deletes a file permanently.
  • rm -r secret_folder — (Remove Recursive) Deletes a folder and everything inside of it.
⚠️ The Suicide Command: rm -rf /
If you ever see a troll on Reddit or a forum telling you to run sudo rm -rf / to "fix your internet," DO NOT DO IT. This command tells the terminal to recursively force-delete the entire Root directory. It will literally delete your entire operating system while it is running.

Once you memorize these five commands, you will realize that moving files via the terminal is vastly faster than dragging and dropping them with a mouse.
