Desktop Environments: The Face of Linux

You don't like how it looks? Rip it out and replace it. | 👤 By JR Nation

On Windows and macOS, the graphical user interface (GUI) is permanently superglued to the core of the operating system. If you absolutely hate the Windows 11 taskbar, your only option is to complain on Reddit and pray Microsoft eventually listens.

Linux doesn't play by those rules. The graphical interface is literally just another piece of software running on top of the engine. This visual layer is called the Desktop Environment (DE). It provides your taskbar, your wallpaper, your window borders, your settings menu, and your default apps. The best part? You can uninstall it, swap it out, or even run three completely different ones on the exact same computer without formatting your drive.

1. The Titans: GNOME vs. KDE Plasma

If you install a modern, heavyweight Linux distribution, you are almost guaranteed to be using one of these two giants. They represent the two main opposing philosophies of Linux design.

GNOME (The macOS Philosophy) KDE Plasma (The Windows Philosophy)
Clean & Focused: Very few buttons. Designed to get out of your way and force you to focus on your actual work. Infinitely Customizable: If you can see it, you can change it. Packed with features, widgets, and ridiculously deep settings menus.
Keyboard-Driven: Relies heavily on the Super (Windows) key and dynamic workspace navigation. Mouse-Driven: Traditional Start Menu, system tray, and taskbar layout. Instantly familiar to Windows refugees.
Default on: Ubuntu, Fedora, Pop!_OS Default on: SteamOS (Steam Deck), EndeavourOS

2. The Lightweights: XFCE & Cinnamon

If you are running an older laptop, or if you want to squeeze every single drop of frame rate out of your gaming rig, you don't want a heavy Desktop Environment eating up your RAM in the background.

  • Cinnamon: The flagship of Linux Mint. It strikes the perfect balance. It looks modern, acts exactly like Windows 7, but is significantly lighter than KDE or GNOME.
  • XFCE: The legendary workhorse. It is extremely lightweight and fast. It might look a bit dated out of the box, but with a few custom themes, it can look incredible while using almost zero system resources.

3. How to Swap Your Desktop Environment

Want to try KDE Plasma but you are currently on Ubuntu (which uses GNOME)? You don't need to reinstall your entire operating system. You just ask your terminal to fetch it from the internet.

Example: Installing KDE Plasma on Debian/Ubuntu

sudo apt install kde-plasma-desktop

Once the terminal finishes doing its thing, reboot your computer. At the login screen, look for a "Gear" or "Session" icon next to your password box. Click it, select "Plasma" from the drop-down menu, and log in. Boom. You are now in a completely new environment.

⚠️ Warning on Distro Hopping & Clutter: Installing multiple DEs installs all their default apps too (meaning you might end up with 3 different calculator apps and 2 text editors cluttering your menu). If you try a DE and hate it, cleanly rip it out using the terminal:

sudo apt autoremove --purge kde-plasma-desktop

4. The Engine Room: X11 vs. Wayland

While the Desktop Environment is the "face" of your system, the "Display Server" is the engine physically drawing the pixels on your monitor. For 30 years, Linux used an engine called X11.

Today, the entire Linux ecosystem is shifting to a faster, more secure, and significantly smoother engine called Wayland. Most modern DEs (like GNOME and KDE Plasma) default to Wayland now, resulting in tear-free video playback, better trackpad gestures, and vastly superior multi-monitor support (especially if your monitors have different refresh rates).

5. A Glimpse into Window Managers

Once you master Desktop Environments, you might notice screenshots of hyper-minimalist, keyboard-only setups on forums like r/unixporn. Those power-users aren't using a full DE; they are using a Window Manager (WM) like Hyprland, i3, or bspwm.

A WM strips away the heavy taskbar, the Bluetooth menus, and the Wi-Fi applets, leaving you with pure, raw terminal power and keyboard shortcuts. We will dive deep into this later in the Pro section of The Linux Hub.

6. Frequently Asked Questions

If I switch my Desktop Environment, will I lose my files?

Not at all. Your personal files (Documents, Downloads, Games) live securely in your /home directory. The DE only changes how your screen looks and operates. Your actual files remain completely untouched.

Can I make Linux look exactly like Windows or macOS?

Absolutely. By using KDE Plasma (for a Windows look) or GNOME/XFCE (for a macOS look), you can install custom icon packs, global themes, and dock applications to perfectly mimic other operating systems. Check out our Ricing Guide to learn how.
