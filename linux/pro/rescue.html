The Rescue Mission: Chroot

When your system dies, you don't bury it. You revive it from where it actually died. | 👤 By JR Nation

You ran an update, your power went out midway through, and now your computer boots to a terrifying black screen. The system is dead. But your files are still perfectly intact on the hard drive. You just need a way to get inside and fix the broken gears.

To do this, we use a technique called Chroot (Change Root). You are going to boot your PC from a USB stick, crack open your broken hard drive, and magically teleport your terminal inside of it. From there, you can fix the system from the inside out.

  • 2. Step 2: Locating the Body (lsblk)
  • 3. Step 3: Mounting the Partitions

1. Step 1: The Live USB

Grab a USB stick with a Linux ISO on it (Ubuntu, Arch, Mint—it doesn't matter which one, as long as it gives you a terminal). Plug it into your broken PC, turn it on, and mash your BIOS key to boot from the USB instead of your dead hard drive.

Once you are on the Live USB desktop, open a terminal. You are now running an operating system entirely out of your RAM.

2. Step 2: Locating the Body (lsblk)

You need to figure out exactly what your dead hard drive partitions are called. Run the list block devices command:

$ lsblk

# Look for your main drive. It will usually be called something like /dev/sda or /dev/nvme0n1.

Identify which partition is your Root (/) partition (usually the biggest one), and which one is your tiny EFI/Boot partition (usually around 500MB).

3. Step 3: Mounting the Partitions

Now, we connect the dead drive to our live USB environment. We mount the Root partition first, and then the Boot partition inside of it.

$ sudo mount /dev/nvme0n1p2 /mnt

# Replace nvme0n1p2 with YOUR specific Root partition.


$ sudo mount /dev/nvme0n1p1 /mnt/boot/efi

# Replace nvme0n1p1 with YOUR specific Boot partition.

4. Step 4: The Infiltration (chroot)

Your broken system is now physically attached to the /mnt folder. It's time to go inside. If you are using an Arch Linux Live USB, they provide a magical script that handles all the complex background linking for you:

$ sudo arch-chroot /mnt

(If you are using a Debian/Ubuntu Live USB, you just type: sudo chroot /mnt)

Your terminal prompt will instantly change. You are no longer controlling the USB stick. Your terminal believes it is natively running inside your broken installation. You have absolute Root authority over the dead drive.

5. Step 5: Open Heart Surgery

Now that you are inside, you can fix whatever broke the system. You have internet access (passed through from the USB), and you can use all your normal package managers.

  • Did a bad update break the system? Just re-run your package manager to finish the broken update (e.g., pacman -Syu or apt upgrade).
  • Did you mess up a config file? Use nano to open the file and fix your typo.
  • Did GRUB completely explode? Reinstall it and regenerate the config file right here in the terminal.
✅ The Escape: Once you have fixed the problem, do not just rip the USB out. You must exit gracefully.
  1. Type exit or press Ctrl + D to leave the chroot environment.
  2. Unmount the drives: sudo umount -R /mnt
  3. Type reboot and remove your USB stick.

If you did the surgery correctly, your computer will boot up exactly as it was before the crash. You just saved yourself hours of reinstalling and setting up your system from scratch. You are officially a Linux God.
